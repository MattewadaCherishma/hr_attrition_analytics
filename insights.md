What I found
1.Overtime is the biggest red flag.
When I looked at who's leaving, one thing stood out immediately — people working overtime quit way more often than people who don't. It's not even close. This makes intuitive sense too: if someone's constantly overworked, they're going to start looking elsewhere.

2.Sales is quietly the riskiest department.
At first glance you'd think R&D has the attrition problem since it has the most people leaving in raw numbers. But R&D also has way more employees overall. When you actually compare it as a percentage, Sales loses a bigger share of its team than R&D does. Small team, bigger problem.

3.Most people who are going to leave, leave early.
The tenure chart made this pretty obvious — attrition is high in the first year or two, and then it drops off and stays low. Basically, if someone sticks around past the early stage, they're probably going to stay for a while. The company's real risk window is right at the start.

What I'd actually recommend

1.Look into workloads on overtime-heavy teams - People working overtime are leaving at a noticeably higher rate. Before it turns into a bigger retention problem, it's worth checking if certain teams are just understaffed and burning people out.

2.Dig into what's going on in Sales specifically - The attrition rate there is higher than you'd expect given the team size. Could be commission structure, could be a manager issue, could be something else — but it's worth talking to a few people who left to figure out why.

3.Fix the onboarding experience - Since most people who leave do it early on, that's the window to focus on. A proper 90-day check-in process or a mentor for new hires could catch people who are already checked out before they actually quit.
